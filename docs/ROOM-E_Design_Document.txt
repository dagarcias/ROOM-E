ROOM-E
Product Design Document
UX/UI Direction, Wireframes, Design System, and User Flow Map
Design intent
ROOM-E should feel like a calm household command center with the speed of a delivery app. The visual language combines the clean dashboard clarity of modern task-management products with mobile commerce patterns inspired by Rappi: quick scanning, strong cards, fast actions, obvious hierarchy, and lightweight decision making.
Version
v1.0
Language: English
Scope: Mobile app
Platform: iOS-first
1. Executive Summary
ROOM-E is a roommate coordination app built around one shared space: the House. The product brings together chores, shared expenses, shopping, chat, and polls into a single mobile experience that reduces friction in everyday co-living.
This document defines the design direction for the product and consolidates four outputs in one handoff-ready artifact: core UX/UI direction, screen wireframes, design system foundations, and user flow maps.

Primary design goals
• Make household coordination feel fast and low-friction.
• Reduce ambiguity around money, tasks, and decisions.
• Keep the app useful even when users open it for only a few seconds.
• Present shared data in a way that avoids blame-heavy UX.
Non-goals for v1
• Detailed analytics dashboards.
• Complex gamification or roommate scoring.
• Heavy customization during onboarding.
• Desktop-first behaviors or enterprise collaboration patterns.
2. Product Experience Principles
One-glance clarity
Users should understand the state of the house in under 5 seconds from the Home screen.
Fast action over deep navigation
Adding a task, expense, or poll should be one obvious action away.
Shared ownership, not surveillance
The product should encourage coordination without feeling punitive.
Operational calm
Visual design should lower stress through structured spacing, soft surfaces, and controlled color use.
Commerce-grade efficiency
Borrow Rappi-like speed patterns where they improve discovery, prioritization, and repeat use.

4. UX/UI Direction
Overall visual tone
ROOM-E should sit between productivity calm and everyday utility energy. The app should feel more premium and composed than a generic household utility, but more approachable and practical than a formal finance product.
Use more of this
Avoid this
• Rounded cards with gentle elevation
• Short section labels and high-contrast totals
• Quick action chips and segmented filters
• Selective use of color to signal priority or status
• Comfortable whitespace with compact but not crowded rows
• Dense spreadsheet-like layouts
• Overly playful roommate scoring mechanics
• Too many saturated colors on the same screen
• Multi-step creation flows for simple actions
• Chat-first layout that hides operational actions
Rappi-inspired patterns to adopt
• Card-led home composition for fast scanning.
• Category chips for common actions and quick filtering.
• Strong primary CTA placement for repeat behaviors.
• Practical use of icons, badges, and counts to reduce reading effort.
• Actionable surfaces: tapping a card should advance the task immediately.
Rappi-inspired patterns to reinterpret carefully
• Do not replicate commerce clutter; ROOM-E should stay calmer and more deliberate.
• Avoid too many promotional surfaces or excessive banners.
• Use urgency cues sparingly; household management is frequent but not always high-intensity.
• Preserve trust by keeping expenses and balances simple and transparent.

6.2 Typography
Display / screen titles: Aptos Display or SF Pro Display equivalent, 24–28 pt.
Section headers: 18–20 pt semibold.
Card titles: 15–17 pt semibold.
Body text: 14–15 pt regular in the product UI.
Metadata / helper text: 12–13 pt regular.
Use bold for totals, ownership, and primary labels only.
6.3 Spacing and layout
Base spacing scale: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, 32.
Primary screen side padding: 20 px.
Vertical rhythm on stacked cards: 12–16 px.
Card internal padding: 16 px default, 20 px for summary cards.
Bottom navigation height should remain comfortable for thumb use.
6.4 Shape, elevation, and motion
Large cards: 20–24 px corner radius.
Inputs and chips: 14–18 px radius.
Use soft shadows sparingly; rely on surface contrast first.
Animations should be short and practical: 150–220 ms for state changes.
Completion interactions should feel tactile but not playful to the point of distraction.

6.6 Accessibility baseline
Meet WCAG-friendly contrast targets for body text and key controls.
Do not rely on color alone for task or payment states; pair with labels or icons.

9. Recommendations for the First Visual Prototype
Prototype only the P0 and P1 screens listed above; avoid exploring edge-case screens too early.
Start with Home, Tasks, Add Task, Expenses, and Add Expense as the first cohesive set.
Validate whether users understand balances and task ownership without explanation.
Test card density on smaller iPhones before polishing micro-details.
Keep the first prototype in Spanish or bilingual UI if that matches target users, but preserve this document as the English source of truth.
10. Open Questions
Should settlement be part of MVP or remain a later enhancement?
Will the House support multiple shopping lists or a single shared list?
Do polls remain inside Chat only, or also surface on Home when active?
Should recurring task rotation be automatic in v1 or manual in the first release?
Will notifications prioritize reminders, financial updates, or poll activity?
11. Final Design Summary
ROOM-E should differentiate itself by feeling organized like a task app, fast like a utility app, and human like a shared home tool. The strongest direction is not to imitate Rappi literally, but to borrow its best mobile behaviors: obvious actions, card-first scanning, and low-effort repetition.
If executed well, the result will be a mobile product that feels practical enough for daily use, clear enough for shared trust, and distinct enough to stand out from generic roommate or task apps.
